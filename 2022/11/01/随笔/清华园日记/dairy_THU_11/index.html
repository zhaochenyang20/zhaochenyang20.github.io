by Ludwig Josef Johann Wittgenstein

昔者苍颉作书，而天雨粟、鬼夜哭。——《淮南子·本经训》

People Revered The Gods And, Therefore, The Written Words

Language and written words have had a sacred place in my heart since before I was exposed to computer science or even before I entered elementary school. I am of Qiang ethnicity and grew up in a minority area on the border of the Sichuan-Tibet Plateau when I was a little boy. At that time, I could feel the reverence of my homeland townies for the written word.

My grandmother used to tell me about the rules of the old days. People revered the gods and, therefore, the written words. Suppose you see a piece of paper with words on the roadside, whether it is in Tibetan or Chinese, you have to wipe your hands, pick it up carefully, put it into the cleanest pocket on your body, carry it home, and then burn it in front of the Buddha, and pray with devotion. If a child does not know what to do and steps on a piece of paper with written words on it, they will be reprimanded by the family’s elders.

In such an atmosphere, I grew up with a reverence for language and written words, knowing that they represented holiness.

From the age of 6, I began to learn linguistics systematically. I studied Tibetan, Qiang, Chinese, and English. At the same time, I read a lot of language and literature, which made me fall in love with this subject and planted my ambition to become a writer.

When I entered university, I studied computer science, which seemed the opposite of my dream of becoming a writer. However, computer science and language and literature are consistent. I realized that many people before me in the field of computer science were also excellent writers—for example, Jun Wu, Paul Graham, and others. One of the people I admire most is Chip Huyen, a computer scientist at Stanford University.

I’m Chip Huyen, a writer, and computer scientist. I grew up chasing grasshoppers in a small rice-farming village in Vietnam.

I’m a co-founder of Claypot AI, a platform for real-time machine learning. Previously, I built machine learning tools at NVIDIA, Snorkel AI, Netflix, and Primer.

Very fortunately, I have a very similar life experience to her. I grew up chasing grasshoppers in a small potatoes-farming village in Sichuan-Tibet Plateau. In addition, I also insisted on documenting my college life in words. My personal blog has accumulated over 70,000 visitors. What’s most impressive is that I am also obsessed with machine learning, especially natural language processing.

Last night’s Q&A and next semester’s course selection

Last night, I helped someone solve a fib problem in the Q&A workshop and realized that my freshman-year fundamentals sucked.

The problem was to compute fib for k numbers less than $10^9$, which is actually very simple. Yet I just remembered that I should even use memory recursion.

Also, I opened an array of 1e9 to store the memory, and the compiler blew up immediately. In fact, for C++, the maximum is 1e7, and you have to determine if the access is out of bounds (Python must have spoiled me, I hardly ever think about space size anymore).

I feel a lot better about the program, which is probably a benefit of writing readme for a long time, but C++ is really not very good for me.

This course is basically over, and my pre is still doing well. The overall effect can be referred to as follows.

The teacher asked me two questions.

  1. give one example from China and abroad to discuss the layoffs of specific companies.
  2. There have been many questions about SenseTime burning too much money. How do I feel about it?

For question 1, I gave the example of my company and Twitter. In the case of Twitter, I answered that the layoffs of big companies must give confidence to Wall Street. Thus, news of big company layoffs is generally obtained from Wall Street. In addition, Musk will certainly reinvent Twitter, but he directly pulled down the bottom line of Silicon Valley layoffs. Capitalists like Musk, who have no moral bottom line, have dealt a devastating blow to Silicon Valley’s startup and engineering culture.

Question 2 I discussed the nature of SenseTime - Tangzi is not an internet company but a technology company. The core profit of the Internet company is converting flows to cash. But Tangzi hopes that artificial intelligence will empower the first or second industries, so this profit model is different. However, the orders in the field of vision are basically from the government. With MEGVII, the market is already saturated. However, NLP and the intelligent city group have yet to land. It depends on who happens first when Tangzi’s technology lands and the capital market loses confidence.

However, considering chatGPT’s colossal success and GPT-4 will appear soon, I am still looking forward to the landing of NLP.

I felt I lacked sound logic when answering, but my teammates said I did a good job. What the heck, that’s it for this class. I then solve the PPT and learn a lesson. It is good enough.

Finally, the teacher also asked an outrageous question: the impact of World War III on the Internet.

I replied that many of the most cutting-edge technology applications are in the military field. However counterintuitive this sounds, it is. Both Tsinghua and UIUC have a large number of military-sponsored programs. War promotes rapid technological development, such as cryptography, computer networks, and architecture, but wreaks havoc on the private economy. Thus, the Internet would be struck by destruction, but computers would grow exponentially.
